From the Field to the Dashboard: How Agriculture Sparked My Data Science Journey

People are sometimes surprised when they hear I started out as an agriculture engineer, not a data analyst. But looking back, the line between the two is straighter than it seems — I just didn't know it yet while I was out in the field in Nepal.

Roots in the field

I trained as an agriculture engineer at Tribhuvan University, and after graduating I joined Muktinath Krishi Company as a Senior Mechanisation Officer. The job was hands-on in every sense: assessing seed processing units, designing drip irrigation layouts, planning greenhouse installations, and helping farmers and dealers get the most out of their machinery. I delivered training sessions to over a hundred farmers and field technicians, and I genuinely loved the work.

But the part of the job that stuck with me most wasn't the machinery — it was the spreadsheets.

The moment data clicked

To support our pricing and distribution strategy, I started building dashboards in Power BI and Excel to track machinery sales, demand trends, and seasonal patterns. What surprised me wasn't the technical side of it — it was watching decisions change because of what the dashboards showed. We could see exactly where demand was shifting and adjust accordingly, and it contributed to a 25% increase in sales. Around the same time, the data we presented to government bodies helped make the case for policy and subsidy changes that lifted youth participation in agriculture by 20%.

That was the moment it clicked for me: the machinery had always been the visible part of the job, but the numbers behind it were what actually moved things. I wanted to get a lot better at that second part.

The leap to Canberra

So I made a decision that felt big at the time — leaving a field I'd trained years for, moving countries, and starting again as a student. I enrolled in a Master of Data Science at the University of Canberra, studying everything from pattern recognition and machine learning to database management, business intelligence, and computer vision.

It wasn't always a smooth transition. Coming from an engineering and field-operations background, a lot of the statistical and programming concepts were genuinely new to me. But the same instinct that drove me to build those first dashboards back in Nepal kept me going — I wanted to understand not just how to analyse data, but how to make it useful to people who don't think in queries and models.

Where I am now

After finishing my degree, I deliberately started close to the ground again — this time at Access Canberra as a Customer Service Officer, working directly with the government systems and data that affect everyday people. It gave me a first-hand view of how data moves (and sometimes gets stuck) inside a large organisation, which has turned out to be just as valuable as anything I learned in a lecture theatre.

Today I work as a Data Analyst with the ACT Government's Data Insights and Analytics team, building Power BI dashboards, validating data with SQL, and — much like those early machinery dashboards — trying to make sure the numbers actually change a decision, not just decorate a report. I'm also Microsoft PL-300 certified and currently completing a Diploma in Project Management alongside the day job.

It's a strange and pretty satisfying loop: I went from building dashboards to help farmers sell machinery, to studying data science formally, to now building dashboards that help a government directorate make better calls. Different fields, same underlying belief — that good data, presented well, changes what people do next.
